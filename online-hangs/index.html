Online Hangs
Websites, tools and platforms for connecting, co-creating, exploring together online

What is this document?

This document contains a collection of tools for connecting online together. It’s a sampling of the many tools that exist, including musical collaboration sites, drawing sites, online galleries, weird experiments, corporate video-meeting tools, and office tools repurposed as spaces for gathering together.

I made this list and hosted a series of meetups where we explored some of them together.

What these tools have in common

  • Invite people to interact together online through typing, voice, video, drawing, music, or any expression that feels meaningful
  • Priority to tools accessed for free through a browser
  • Priority to the experimental, weird, home-made, simple, or barely working (anything with any interesting features is welcome)

A rambling section about my relationship to online spaces

I’ve always been curious about spaces for online connection. I mean, I’m infinitely curious about connection and online connection is no exception. 

The first time I entered an online chatroom I was mystified by the stream of $trang3 u$3rnam3$ echoing an indecipherable question: a/s/l? The correct response seemed to be 18/f/Cali. This response was the key to understanding the both the question and the reason for asking. 

My friend would always lie. She was lying for her own sake, to imagine and breathe life into a new possible self, a fantasy roleplay. I didn’t understand why a/s/l mattered. To me the curious glory of the thing was presence without shared embodiment, touching minds without seeing or being seen, co-existence in an imaginary space that we could co-create with our minds and words. The a/s/l question felt so irrelevant and diminishing of the possibility space I felt I must be from another planet.

Later I got into ICQ. One day a friend messaged me about something I had never told him about and in one horrifying breath I realized that he had found some impossible hack and could see what I was typing before I hit send. In those days, I typed and I thought and edited, and deleted and even wrote things down to see if I felt they were true. I thought that the text area was a sacred space of privacy and contemplation, only launched into the world as I hit send. 

Then there was LiveJournal. Then building an online community with no budget and duct-taping together a world for us online with whatever free tools I could find, all the while feeling deep gut-terror about privacy and building our spaces in the back passageways of whatever corporate freeware we could find.
