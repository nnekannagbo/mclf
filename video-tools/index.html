Video Tools:
Meeting online for teachers, artists, and non-profits

We just suddenly found ourselves in a situation where everything involving other people needs to be remote. How do we choose our tools?

I’ve been using video conferencing tools for a decade: organizing volunteers all over the country and collaborating with people all over the world. I work with technology, design, and people for a living. I’ve organized over a thousand online video meetings. I'm sorry to say: there isn't a single best tool.

All we can do is understand the needs of our groups, make a decision together, and try things out.

Choosing a tool

Things you may consider when choosing a tool for video calls:

  • Price
  • Security & privacy
  • Number of participants
  • Ease of use
  • Stability
  • Works with different devices and browsers
  • Accessibility
  • Closed captions
  • Low Bandwidth
  • Screen share
  • Breakaway rooms
  • Chat
  • Archive
  • Free and open-source software

Test Notes

  • Jitsi Video Conferencing: Free Open Source Option. Very easy to join and set up a call. Nice features like “hand up” in chat. Connection is unfortunately heavy on older computers. Ability to join by phone, sounded better than the test with Google, but still not great.
  • Google Meet: Popular, currently free for the business version? I couldn’t create a meeting with my normal account, I needed to use my nonprofit account. I had to approve each attendee manually as they joined the call. Closed captioning was great! It also helped me to slow down and enunciate. Very easy to join and use. Ability to join by phone but it sounded like everyone was underwater.
  • Zoom: Popular, unfortunately with many privacy and security concerns. Downloadable application. Participants can also join in-browser without accounts (though none of our testers managed it). Audio didn’t work when someone tried to join with Firefox. Whiteboard feature. 40 minutes max for free accounts (as many as you want, you can daisy chain them).
  • Skype: Popular. Can send a link instead of having participants make an account? Many people already have the software. One of the major problems is that people seem to get locked out of their accounts frequently, and getting access again seems basically impossible. The only solution is to make an entirely new account and this has a large amount of overhead psychologically and logistically.
  • Whereby: (formerly Appear.in) Restricted for 4 people for small calls. Very easy to jump into. I used this years ago when meeting with new people because there is no overhead for a new user. I have spent hundreds of hours trying to help new users troubleshoot Google Hangouts and Skype, so the lack of overhead (click and it works) is invaluable to me. Hilariously the link stays the same so if one meeting runs late the next person might just join you.
  • Google Hangouts: Group video-conferences, including screen-sharing for 10 guests (150 for non-video).
  • Discord: 9 video participants (50 viewers?) and a high user overhead for signup.
  • Jami: Free Open Source Option. Downloaded software to computer. The UX is bad. Crashed repeatedly. I’m cheering for this one but it’s not usable for me right now.
  • Facebook Messenger Video Chat: 50 video participants, need Facebook account. Very minimal, no screenshare.
  • Uberconference: seemed to require someone to dial America. Test again!

Quick tips on video calls

  • Welcome people when they arrive. Treat the virtual space as an actual space.
  • Have a buffer period at the beginning of the meeting specifically for tech problems (15 minutes the first time you use a tool). Remind people as they arrive so they don’t feel lost. Run through video and microphone and features if there are no problems.
  • Be mindful of how you share your join link. Uninvited guests may appear to do some performance art or troll your meeting if you publically share the link.
  • Discuss your needs with audio and video (on or off). Keeping video and audio on are wonderful for engagement but come with major issues (people might feel uncomfortable, accidental sharing, distracting noise, heavy internet use, accessibility issues).
  • Have a single stable platform for communication while people set up the software or program (email, text, messenger etc).
