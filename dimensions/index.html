Dimensions of Online Spaces:
10 Considerations when choosing or designing online spaces

A year ago when the pandemic pushed us online all the time, I made a list of standard video tools for meeting with others online.

Since then, I’ve make a list of more experimental spaces for online hangs, hosted meetups where we explore online collaborative spaces together, and even shared some of these spaces on national morning news radio.

In this work I’ve noticed some interesting dimensions in spaces for connecting online. These dimensions may be useful for people who are designing online spaces. They may also be useful to anyone who is considering what kinds of spaces they want to use or join.

I try to give extreme examples to show the edges of the dimensions, but they are mostly spectrums of possibility, rather than binary oppositions.

Scheduled <<<>>> Spontaneous

  • Scheduled Spaces are designed for gatherings that happen at specific times. For example, a weekly Zoom reading group.
  • Spontaneous Spaces are designed for unplanned connections, allowing you to show up at any time. For example Chatroulette (not safe for work) where you can hop on and connect with strangers any time.

Event organizers often prefer to schedule gatherings so they can navigate availability, more tightly manage attendees, and handle security problems. Scheduling can be useful for participants as well, building consistency, intimate community, and feelings of anticipation.

Desire is growing for online spaces that resemble offline spaces (pubs, swimming pools, arcades, libraries) in flexibly meeting peoples real-time needs and desires. Many attendees often want to manage their social energy in real-time, deciding moment-to-moment what they have the energy and capacity to do.

Synchronous <<<>>> Asynchronous

  • Synchronous Spaces require people to be online at the same time. They might be spontaneous and they might be scheduled, but they are always real-time, or as close to real time as the technology and the human brain allows. For example, Google Meet or any other video meeting.
  • Asynchronous Spaces allow people to engage at their own convenience. A good example is: twitter, or long email exchanges.

Synchronous spaces can be great for generating social energy, making space for dynamic connections, and rapid feedback. Asynchronous spaces are more like the online version of writing letters, allowing more time for processing information or careful consideration.

Temporary <<<>>> Permanent

  • Temporary Spaces remove content from view after a certain amount of time. A popular example is SnapChat. Some platforms are even self-deleting, deleting the “room” or “document” after a period of inactivity, like HackPad.
  • Permanent Spaces keep content up forever. An example of this is a message board, where posts can live on as long as someone pays the server fees.

Permanent spaces can help people share and transfer knowledge across generations and social bubbles. This can be useful, like a message board around a specific illness allowing people to surface shared experience and share tactics across decades. Temporary spaces are increasingly popular as they can help ease anxiety around the temporal relevance of content.

Centralized <<<>>> Decentralized <<<>>> Distributed

  • Centralized Spaces are organized so everyone is connected to a central hub or owner.
  • Decentralized Spaces are spaces with multiple central hubs or owners.
  • Distributed Spaces are organized so everyone is able to share ownership of the space.

One way to think about this dimension is in terms of the actual servers that host the space. Another way to think about it is in terms of the organization of the space itself.

Public <<<>>> Community <<<>>> Private

  • Public Spaces are designed for connections between strangers. A good example is Dialup, an app that matches you with a random stranger over the phone to discuss a specific topic.
  • Community Spaces are designed to connect you with a broader community. One example might be a Discord group around a specific festival or topic. Another example is the way conversations on Twitter or Facebook include the friends of your friends.
  • Private Spaces are designed specifically for connection with friends, family, and intimate connection. Example of this might be a Signal group chat, or the friends-only social network, Cocoon.

Public spaces are probably the riskiest spaces, but they can also be the most exciting. Community spaces often allow for the adventure of connecting with strangers, with the safety rail of a shared contact or shared community. Private spaces are ooo.

Spatial <<<>>> Flat

  • Spatial Spaces use the space between your avatar and the avatars of other people as a meaningful input. They often have “rooms” or “maps”. For example, there are a number of spaces online that have spatial audio, so you can only hear and/or see people that you are close to.
  • Flat Spaces treat all participants identically. A good example is when you see every participant in a video call in identical boxes, and hear all participants equally, like Skype.

Text-Only <<<>>> Voice-first <<<>>> Video

  • Text-Only Spaces are designed to be used only with written text.
  • Voice-first Spaces allow people to communicate using their voices. An example is Cozy Room, an online gathering space where you are represented by a little character and you see their mouth moving as you speak.
  • Video Spaces allow you to see others and be seen. Some spaces might only allow others to see you, like Twitch or YouTube. Some spaces might allow everyone to see everyone, like any video call. The far extreme of video connection would be a body-rigged live-cam VR hangout. These spaces allow people to communicate with their bodies in a way that feels the most natural to many people.

Text-only spaces often have a lower environmental impact and less accessibility issues. Some people find it easier to communicate over text than over video for a number of different reasons. Video spaces allow people to communicate with their bodies in a way that feels the most natural to many people.

Moderated <<<>>> Unmoderated

  • Structured Spaces have a set of rules that are enforced by special users. If you break the rules you are penalized or kicked out. Some spaces are community moderated, where anyone is able to kick anyone out, or users are kicked out by vote. For example, that drawing game where you vote to remove someone.
  • Sandbox Spaces have no rules. Of course, all spaces have unspoken rules and power structures, so unmoderated spaces can be understood as secretly moderated spaces (Tyranny of structurelessness). Unmoderated spaces can be useful when you are curious about the state of the community, they communicate a lot about who is present.

OOO

Structured <<<>>> Sandbox

  • Structured Spaces have strict or pre-designed structure with very little creative freedom.
  • Sandbox Spaces are created as you go, sometimes offering creative tools but mostly relying on participants to create meaning. A good example is minecraft.

OOO

Ambient <<<>>> Direct

  • Ambient Spaces are spaces where you spend a lot of passive time without connecting with others. An example is a co-working Discord channel, or a Zoom call with the sound muted.
  • Direct Spaces are spaces that require full attention and interaction with others. An example is a one-on-one video meeting.

Ambient spaces can provide feelings of connection and community without asking much of participants. Like spontaneous spaces, they allow for more flexibility and responsiveness to meet the needs of people. Direct spaces are better for focussed connection between smaller groups, and are nearly always also designed for scheduled gatherings.

Curated <<<>>> Open

  • Curated Spaces only allow for certain kinds of content. Usually there are single figureheads or tight groups of people who define and decide what is welcome in the space. These spaces tend to foster passionate communities.
  • Open Spaces are open to any kind of content you want to throw at them. Because of the lack of cohesiveness, they often have many people coming and going.

OOO

Used-As-Intended <<<>>> Repurposed

  • Used-As-Intended Spaces are used more or less for their designed purpose. Of course, design often shifts with use, so this is a bit flexible. A good example is Twitter: designed for microblogging, and used more or less for this purpose.
  • Repurposed Spaces are spaces designed for a specific use that are repurposed for other kinds of use. A good example of this is Google Spreadsheet Parties.

OOO

Accessibled <<<>>> Inaccessible

  • Accessible Spaces are spaces that people can access. Of course, there is no space that all people can access, so like all the other dimensions, this is a spectrum. More accessible spaces might take into account features like captions, read-aloud text, support for different languages, platforms that work with low-internet, simple and no-flashing visuals, flexibility around camera use, and space for people to show up as they are and be welcomed.
  • Inaccessible Spaces are spaces that block access. Often this involves expecting people to use the platform in a rigid way, making no allowances for differences in experiences and environments. Questions around inaccessibility might start with: who has access? Who doesn’t have access? Who has partial access? Why?

OOO

Collaborative <<<>>> Competitive

  • Collaborative Spaces are designed for many people to contribute, often in interactive and cumulative ways. Examples include fanfic communities or collaborative whiteboards for online drawing.
  • Competitive Spaces encourage competition by design. For example, most online games are competitive spaces.

OOO

Humanizing <<<>>> Dehumanizing

  • Humanizing Spaces flatten people into “other” or simple representations.
  • Dehumanizing Spaces invite complexity.

OOO

Anonymous <<<>>> Persistent Identity <<<>>> Video

  • Anonymous Spaces allow you to participate without identifying yourself to other participants (you are never completely anonymous online). For example, an online text editor that allows you to collaboratively write a story with friends without knowing who wrote which part. Anonymous spaces can be enormously liberating, especially if you feel pressure to behave in a certain way.
  • Persistent Identity Spaces are on the other end of the spectrum, forcing you to verify and publish your username with a passport. Sometimes this is necessary for legal reasons. They often exclude people who don’t have documentation or have a different name than the documentation.
  • Onymous Spaces allow you to create a name of your choice, but encourage you to use only that name, designing for a singular persistent identity. Persistent usernames can increase accountability and allow for richer relationships.

OOO

Privacy-violating <<<>>> Privacy-first

  • Privacy-violating generally exist to collect and sell your data and social graphs to advertisers. An excellent example of this is Facebook. This is worth mentioning because it’s often the financial model of connecting spaces.
  • Privacy-first only collect data with your informed consent, and only uses this to make the experience better for you. For example, inviting you to share your location to match you with other local stamp collectors.

OOO

Creation <<<>>> Consumption

  • Creation Spaces inherently invite you to actively contribute creatively to the space. This might be drawing, music, dance, writing, or even creating the environment.
  • Consumption Spaces, in the most absolute sense, don’t belong on this list, because they aren’t really spaces for connecting with other people online. But many spaces invite you to consume more than create while simultaneously allowing for interactivity. An example for this is a Twitch Stream or a Netflix party.

Last Thoughts

There have been platforms for connecting online since the beginning of the internet. The internet itself is a platform for connecting. But since the first wave of COVID we’ve seen a massive emergence of online spaces and experimentation with how people connect online. It’s strange that the social media giants didn’t leap into this opportunity and shoulder everyone else out of the way. They are perfectly positioned, and already have hooks in most of us. But I’m delighted that they didn’t.

Lately I’ve been thinking about how these different platforms vary. About how online spaces shape our connections, and how we might reimagine what online spaces could be.
